Conversations with Robots: Voice, Smart Agents & the Case for Structured Content

1. What is the difference between structured content and semantic HTML?

Structured content is how you organize information into smaller, reusable parts that clearly describe what the content is, no matter where it’s used (for example like in websites, or apps). Semantic HTML is how you show that info on a webpage using meaningful HTML tags, like < header > or < article >, so that people, search engines, and assistive tools know what each part means.

2. How do they work together?

They work together because structured content provides the clean pieces of information, and semantic HTML is what gives those pieces meaning on a webpage. When you combine them, the content is not only organized but also easy for people, search engines, and smart assistants to read and use.

3. Why is it important to have both?

It’s important to have both because structured content makes information clear and reusable, while semantic HTML makes sure that meaning comes through on a webpage. Together, they help search engines, smart agents, and screen readers understand the content, so it’s easier to find, more accessible, and can be used across different platforms.

David McRaney

Hyperbolic discounting means people choose smaller rewards now instead of waiting for bigger ones later on. McRaney says one way to fight this is by pre-committing, like setting a plan or deadline ahead of time. This works well for me because once I set a clear deadline or tell someone else, I feel way more responsible and more likely to stick with it.
